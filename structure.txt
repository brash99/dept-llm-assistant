What We Have Learned
======================================================

Modern RAG systems are not "LLMs with documents."

They are pipelines that progressively transform
information into richer semantic representations.

Each stage has a single responsibility:

    Parser        extracts information.

    Document      preserves semantics and provenance.

    Chunker       creates reasoning units.

    Embedder      maps semantics into geometry.

    Vector DB     indexes semantic space.

    Retriever     finds nearby concepts.

    Re-ranker     improves relevance.

    LLM           performs reasoning over retrieved
                  context.

The power of the system comes from the composition
of these stages, not from any single component.

Finished v0.2.0 - Benchmark-Driven Retrieval Platform

What struck me over the last few days is that we kept saying things like:

“Spreadsheets are different.”

“Policies behave differently.”

“PowerPoints are weird.”

“Titles matter.”

At first those felt like implementation details. Now I think they’re manifestations of something deeper.

A semantic ecosystem

A semantic ecosystem is a community of documents that encode knowledge in a characteristic way.

Not by file type.

Not by department.

By how meaning is represented.

For example:

Ecosystem

Characteristics

Likely Best Retrieval

Policies

Dense prose, precise language, cross-references

Dense embeddings + reranker

Spreadsheets

Sparse text, repeated headers, relational structure

Hybrid / structural retrieval

Course catalogs

Semi-structured, repeated templates

Dense retrieval

Event calendars

Temporal + tabular + names

Hybrid + temporal indexing

Meeting minutes

Narrative with decisions and actions

Dense retrieval

Forms

Boilerplate with a few critical fields

Field-aware retrieval

Presentations

Bullets, fragmented ideas, visual context

Slide-aware chunking

Notice something?

Those aren’t file types.

They’re semantic habitats.

You could have a PDF that belongs to three different ecosystems.

Semantic Ecology

The ecology isn’t the documents.

The ecology is how the ecosystems interact.

For example:

Faculty Handbook
        │
        ▼
Travel Policy
        │
        ▼
Budget Documents
        │
        ▼
Faculty Senate Minutes
        │
        ▼
Strategic Plan

One final thought

There’s a beautiful parallel here with physics.

Early physicists talked about particles because that’s what they could observe. Eventually they realized the interesting behavior came from systems—gases, plasmas, condensed matter, galaxies—where the interactions mattered as much as the individual constituents.

I think we’re seeing the same thing.

At first, we’ve been treating documents as independent “particles.”

Now we’re beginning to see the “condensed matter” of institutional knowledge: collections of documents with shared semantics, conventions, and interactions. The retrieval failures are revealing that structure to us.

If that intuition continues to hold, I wouldn’t be surprised if “semantic ecosystems” becomes one of the organizing concepts of the Institutional Knowledge Framework. It’s memorable, it’s descriptive, and, most importantly, it emerged from observation rather than being imposed from the outset—which is often the hallmark of a useful scientific idea.
